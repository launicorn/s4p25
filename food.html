Bolivian street food finds its place at the fine dining table

From the world’s highest navigable lake, Titicaca, comes a culinary treasure: crispy-skinned fish cooked in a hot copper pan and served with Andean boiled corn and dehydrated potatoes in traditional clay dishes. This signature dish at La Rufina in La Paz was just named one of the world’s best by 50 Best Discovery.

“La Rufina started as a very small kitchen,” says 30-year-old chef Dennis Llusco, a native of the Lake Titicaca region. Opened in 2021, it never imagined two locations or a spot on the 50 Best Discovery list—yet humble street-food roots propelled it onto the global stage.

Stepping through La Rufina feels like a journey through Bolivian traditions. Diners mingle with cholitas—Indigenous women in multilayered skirts and bowler hats—and sample flavors borrowed from street corners and markets, all reimagined as elegant, fine-dining experiences in the heart of La Paz.

The menu highlights local favorites elevated to haute cuisine: baked potatoes stuffed with savory minced-meat stew, anticuchos of grilled beef-heart fillets served with potatoes and yellow chili-pepper sauce, and the Wallake soup made with Lake Titicaca fish—each dish celebrating Bolivia’s street-food heritage.
